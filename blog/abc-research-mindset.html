The ABC Research Mindset

A simple trick to debias yourself when judging new ideas
February 2026 · Chunyuan Deng

I want to share a small thinking tool I've been using lately. I'm not an established researcher—far from it—but I've found this practice genuinely useful for calibrating my own excitement about new ideas. Maybe it'll be useful for you too, or maybe you'll tell me I'm completely off. Either way, let's talk about it.

The Novelty Trap

Here's a thing about us humans: we really like new stuff. There's something almost instinctive about it—a shiny new idea just feels better than the thing we already have. In research, this shows up constantly. The community has method A and method B, and someone proposes method C. We read the paper and think, "oh, this is interesting, this is fresh." But hold on—is it actually better? Or does it just feel that way because it's... new?

Novelty and progress are not the same thing. But our brains have a surprisingly hard time telling them apart.

The ABC Principle

So here's the debiasing trick. It's almost embarrassingly simple.

Say the community already has work A and B, and you (or someone) propose C. Instead of evaluating C in the natural order—"given A and B, how good is C?"—flip it. Imagine a world where we already had A and C, and someone proposes B instead. Now ask yourself: which proposal is actually more compelling?

Formally, compare C | A,B versus B | A,C.

That's it. By swapping the order, you strip away the novelty premium. You're no longer comparing "the new thing" against "the established things"—you're comparing two ideas on equal footing, purely on their merits.

Example I: Latent Reasoning

Let me walk through a concrete example that's been on my mind lately. I want to be upfront: this is my personal reading. Yours might be different, and that's totally fine.

A—Machines naturally reason in latent space. This isn't new at all. Arguably, all of machine learning is about building better representation spaces, finding the best features for next-token prediction. The "latent space" has always been there, quietly doing the heavy lifting.

B—Chain-of-Thought reasoning. Someone proposes that instead of reasoning purely in hidden states, we should have models reason over explicit textual tokens. Step by step, in natural language. This idea reshapes how we think about inference.

C—Latent reasoning (the recent wave), along with interpretability work trying to understand what these latent tokens actually encode.

Now, in the natural order (A,B → propose C): latent reasoning feels exciting. We're going back to continuous representations! We're moving beyond the constraints of text! The novelty is undeniable—working in a continuous latent space is genuinely different from text-based CoT.

But notice what we trade away. CoT's biggest strength is that humans can read it, write it, supervise it, and scale it. Both humans and machines can generate reasoning traces cheaply. Latent tokens? We're fundamentally hard-pressed to write or verify them. So we sacrifice scalability and interpretability for—well, what exactly?

Now flip it. Imagine we live in a world with A and C—machines have always had latent reasoning, and people have been working on its interpretability. Now someone walks in and proposes B: "Hey, what if we make the model reason in plain text?"

Think about that pitch for a second.

Propose C | A, B
Given CoT exists, pitch latent reasoning
Operates in continuous space
Potentially richer representations
Hard to supervise or scale
Humans can't write latent tokens
Needs extra interpretability work
Trades proven gains for unknowns
Propose B | A, C
Given latent reasoning exists, pitch CoT
Humans can read the reasoning
Both humans and machines can write it
It just works—no special decoding
Scales beautifully with more data
Easy to debug and verify
Naturally supports supervision
Fig. 2 — The Latent Reasoning Diptych: compare the two proposals side by side.

Compare the two columns. C | A,B—latent reasoning, given we already have CoT—is asking us to give up concrete, well-understood advantages for a less transparent alternative. B | A,C—CoT, given we already have latent reasoning—offers an elegant, practical, scalable solution that anyone can inspect and improve.

I have my own judgment on which proposal is stronger, and I suspect you might have yours too. The point isn't that one is definitively right—it's that the ABC reordering helps you see past the sheer novelty of "oh, continuous space!" and actually weigh the tradeoffs.

Example II: The Transformer

Here's another one. And honestly, the more I work on this stuff and think about it, the more my respect for it grows.

The Transformer architecture. It's easy to take it for granted—it's been around since 2017, it's everywhere, it's the wallpaper of modern ML. But let's actually sit with what it gives us.

Most architectures are finicky. You need to carefully tune hyperparameters, find the best configuration, cherry-pick settings just to make them competitive. The Transformer? It just... stands there. (My friend @zhouxiang and I once did a lively cosplay of it—long story.) You train it, and it gives you good performance. Remarkably, stubbornly robust.

Fig. 3 — The Transformer, rendered as a classical column. It just stands there, and everything works.

And the properties keep stacking up. Transformers with CoT are theoretically Turing-complete—they can, in principle, compute anything. They maintain exact KV caches, which means fast inference and excellent recall over long contexts. They're a natural fit for post-training, for agentic RL, for all the things people want to build on top—these cherries can be placed safely on a sturdy foundation.

Now apply the ABC test. For any new architecture claiming to sweep aside the Transformer: imagine you already had that new architecture (C), and someone proposed the Transformer (B). What would the pitch sound like?

"I have this architecture that just works out of the box. It scales predictably. It has an exact memory mechanism. It supports arbitrary post-training. You barely need to tune anything. And oh, by the way, paired with textual reasoning, it's theoretically universal."

That's a pretty compelling proposal. For most proposed alternatives, I think the Transformer-as-proposal would actually be the stronger pitch.

This doesn't mean we should stop exploring new architectures—not at all. But it does mean we should be respectful and calm about the baseline we're comparing against. The Transformer earned its place. Acknowledging that isn't defeatism; it's intellectual honesty.

Staying Honest

The ABC principle isn't a rigorous formal framework. It's just a mental habit. A way to pause before getting swept up in the excitement of the new and ask: "Is this actually better, or does it just feel better because it's different?"

I find it keeps me more honest with myself, especially when evaluating my own ideas. (My own ideas feel the most novel to me, which means they need the most debiasing.)

If you have thoughts on this, examples where this principle breaks down, or cases where the ABC test actually reveals the opposite of what you'd expect—I'd love to hear about them. This is very much a work in progress, like everything else in my research life.

This post is a personal reflection, not a claim of authority. If you find the ABC framing useful, feel free to borrow it. If you think it's flawed, I genuinely want to know why.
